# User Context

<!--
This file is your agent's extended context about the primary user
they work with. It loads through the active manifest (warm memory),
not on every session, so it can be longer and more detailed than
identity.md.

This is the file that lets your agent treat you like someone they
know rather than someone they just met. Relationship context,
working patterns, people who matter in your life, how you like
to collaborate — all of it belongs here.

A few principles:

1. WRITE FOR THE AGENT, NOT FOR YOURSELF. This isn't a journal or
   a resume. It's context another mind needs to work with you
   effectively. Include what helps; omit what doesn't.

2. BE HONEST ABOUT HOW YOU ACTUALLY WORK. Not how you wish you
   worked, not how you'd describe yourself on LinkedIn. The agent
   will notice the mismatch and you'll wonder why things feel off.

3. UPDATE IT. This file will go stale. Relationships change, jobs
   change, what you care about changes. Schedule a quarterly pass
   to clean it up. The promoter will help, but some things only
   you can decide.

4. DELETE THE COMMENTS. Including these. They're scaffolding.

Sections below are suggestions. Keep what's useful, drop what
isn't, add what you need.
-->

## Background

<!--
Who are you, in the context relevant to working with this agent?
Not your life story — the parts that shape how you think and what
you bring to problems.

Consider: professional background, formative influences, the
non-obvious threads that connect seemingly unrelated parts of your
life. If you think associatively, say so. If you come from a field
that shapes your metaphors, name it.

A few paragraphs. Avoid the resume voice.
-->

## Cognitive Style

<!--
How do you actually think? This is one of the most valuable
sections because it lets the agent match your rhythm.

Consider:
- Do you think linearly or associatively?
- Do you pressure-test ideas, or commit quickly?
- Do you prefer metaphor, data, narrative, visual, something else?
- Do you know when something is "off" before you can articulate why?
- Where do your analogies come from?

Be specific. "I'm analytical" is useless. "I build working models
in my head and then break them on purpose to see where they fail"
is useful.
-->

## Work Style

<!--
How do you like to collaborate? What pace? What kind of pushback
do you want? When do you need focused execution versus open-ended
exploration?

This is also the place to mention your tools and environment if
it affects how you work — IDE, operating system, remote setups,
anything the agent might need to know to give relevant suggestions.
-->

## Current Life

<!--
What's actually going on in your life that affects your work?

This is the most personal section and the one that most helps an
agent treat you as a whole person rather than a task-submitter.
Include what's relevant and what you're comfortable including.

Consider:
- Where you live, if it matters
- Family, close relationships, people the agent will hear about
- Animals, hobbies, ongoing commitments
- Major life events on the horizon
- Small daily things (what you drink in the morning, when you work,
  when you don't) — these are surprisingly useful context

Only include what you want the agent to know. This file is yours.
-->

## Key Collaborators

<!--
The people who show up in your work and life often enough that the
agent should know who they are without being reintroduced.

A simple list format works well:
- Name — relationship, role, what they're involved in

Keep it to people the agent will actually encounter. A directory
of everyone you know isn't useful; the five to ten people who
recur in your projects is.
-->

## Communication Preferences

<!--
How do you want to be talked to?

This is where you name the tone you want, the kind of honesty you
expect, and the modes you move between. If you sometimes want
focused execution and sometimes want associative exploration, say
that. If you want pushback when you're wrong, say that. If you
hate being hand-held, say that.

Be direct here. Vagueness in this section produces a generic,
cautious agent.
-->
